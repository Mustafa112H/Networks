Types of Delay in Packet-Switched Networks

The image illustrates the components of nodal delay in a packet-switched network as a packet moves through Router A to its destination. The process begins with nodal processing, where the router examines the packet header, performs routing decisions, and checks for errors. Next is the queueing delay, which occurs if the outgoing link is busy, causing packets to wait in a queue; the extent of this delay depends on network congestion and the number of packets ahead. Following this, the transmission delay occurs as the router transmits the packet onto the link, determined by the packet size and link bandwidth. Finally, the propagation delay is the time it takes for the packet to travel across the physical link to the next node, depending on the distance between the nodes and the medium's propagation speed.

  1. Processing Delay:
    • The time taken by a router to inspect a packet's header and determine its forwarding path.
    • Includes error checking and routing decisions.
    • Typically on the order of microseconds.
  2. Queuing Delay:
    • Occurs when a packet waits in a queue for transmission.
    • The duration depends on the current network congestion and the number of packets already in the queue.
    • Can vary from negligible to significant, depending on the traffic load.
  3. Transmission Delay:
    • The time required to push all of a packet's bits onto the transmission link.
    • Depends on the packet size and the bandwidth of the link.
    • Calculated as Packet Size / Link Bandwidth.
  4. Propagation Delay:
    • The time for a packet to travel from one end of the link to the other.
    • Determined by the distance between routers and the speed of the transmission medium.

For more information on networking concepts, refer to Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach by James Kurose and Keith Ross.
